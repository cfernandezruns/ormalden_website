Municipal Platform 2021

Ratified July 29th, 2021

This platform was created after several months of community outreach, polling, research, and internal discussion and debate. It represents our vision for a progressive future for Malden. We believe that our government should work for everyone, regardless of identity or class.

Pass an ordinance that signing a new renter lease will trigger checking for lead pipes.

We want clean drinking water for everyone in Malden.

Currently, the city primarily checks for the presence of lead pipes when homes are sold. Then, lead pipes are scheduled for replacement.

We believe that renters should also be protected from the effects of lead pipes. We want the city to expand its pipe testing policy to include testing when new renters move into an existing building.

Check here if your home is impacted by lead pipes!

Update the Malden Traffic Commission to handle all forms of transportation, not just car traffic.

As our city grows, it's vitally important that our infrastructure grows as well.

This needs to include provisions for many types of transportation, including pedestrians, bicycles, wheelchairs, and busses.

The current Malden Traffic Commission focuses primarily on car traffic, which ignores other modes of transport that are important to a robust traffic plan.

Establish an Inclusive Zoning Policy to add affordable units required by zoning for new developments.

Malden is growing. Malden's population is up about 10% according to 2020 census data.

Affordable housing is one of the most important issues in the greater Boston metro, including here in Malden.

As new dwellings are constructed, we believe that affordable units should be included to help prevent Maldonians from being priced out of their neighborhoods.

Read more on Inclusive Zoning here.

Create a licensing system for mega landlords owning more than 49 units.

Tax these licensed owners at the commercial rate rather than the residential rate. Have licenses revoked if owners fail to upkeep their property to acceptable standards.

We believe that landlords, especially ones controlling large amounts of units, need to be held accountable for failing to maintain their properties.

The city was lucky that the parapet collapse at the Mount Vernon Apartments, thankfully, did not hurt anyone. However, this is a clear example that Malden's landlords need more oversight. Our tenants must be protected from unclean or dangerous conditions caused by landlord neglect.

Additionally, we believe that landlords that are quite obviously running a large scale business by owning dozens of units should be treated and taxed like a business.

Pass an ordinance that all city councilors with personal or familial connections to a given business sector must recuse themselves from legislating on said business area.

Part of the role of the city council is to pass laws regulating private businesses here in Malden. State law already requires that municipal employees are subject to conflict of interest disclosures. We believe that enforcement of these ethics regulations should be strengthened here in Malden.

Read more on municipal conflict of interest law.

Ensure that all city council votes be publicly disclosed and stored in a searchable recordkeeping system. Upgrade the city website to make it fully accessible with full text search for all public information.

Many city records are available through the city's Granicus portal. However, this interface can be difficult to use.

We believe that the city's website should be upgraded to improve the ability for community members to understand what is happening in city government.

Expand the MPS budget to support the increase of substitute, paraprofessional, before-school, and after-school educator pay.

Educators are part of the backbone of society, and they have faced some of the greatest challenges presented by the COVID-19 pandemic. The pandemic has highlighted the need for a robust education system that goes beyond the classroom itself.

Support the increase in hiring and retention of BIPOC educators and staff.

According to 2020 US Census data, less than half of Malden identifies as white alone. Malden public schools are among the most diverse in the state of Massachusetts.

Research has shown that having just one same-race teacher can significantly improve the chances of students graduating high school and enrolling in college.

We believe that representation in education is critically important, and is one of the best ways to strengthen Malden's public schools.

Ensure that all city meetings, forums, and public information are accessible to all communities through high quality translation.

With a city as diverse as ours, it is important that public meetings and documents include translation support to ensure that everyone in our community is able to participate in our democracy.

Pass an ordinance that city and school employees shall not be misclassified as contractors in order to weaken their labor protections.

There has been an industry trend in recent years towards a 'gig economy', where companies hire workers as independent contractors, rather than as employees.

The city of Malden should not treat workers like this. We want the city council to ensure that city workers are treated fairly and not exploited.

Pass an ordinance that everyone drawing a paycheck from the city budget is paid a minimum rate of $15/hour.

We believe that the federal minimum wage should be raised to $15 dollars an hour.

While advocating for that national policy, we believe that at the local level, the city of Malden should do its part by paying everyone a minimum of $15/hour, regardless of classification status.

Pass a Wage Theft Ordinance to protect Malden workers from the most common type of theft.

The most common type of theft, by far, is wage theft. Wage theft occurs when employers fail to pay their employees for all hours worked, fail to pay overtime premiums, ask employees to work through their rest breaks, or to continue doing work when off-the-clock.

The city of Malden should create a process allowing workers to report wage theft violations, and hold violating employers accountable.

Read more on Wage Theft in the US.

Pass an ordinance that new hires shall receive training about their workplace rights and the benefits of unionization.

In the US, it is common for employers to actively resist their employees creating a union. This can include forcing employees to attend anti-union training, distributing anti-union literature, and threatening employees with being fired if they try to form a union.

We believe that the city should help counter corporate propaganda by requiring that all new hires receive training on their workplace rights and the benefits of unionization.

Support the creation of new parks and green spaces, like the Spot Pond Greenway and the Malden River Works.

Creating green public spaces where Maldonians can relax, walk, and exercise is an excellent way to improve the mental and physical health of our city. Building these parks along our river doubles as a way to mitigate the risks of flooding due to climate change.

Read more about the Spot Pond Greenway project.

Learn more about the Malden River Works project.

Protect existing green spaces from development, and support the planting of street trees to lessen the effect of urban heat islands.

Climate change is real, and it is here. We need the city to take active measures to combat the effects of climate change, like the urban heat island effect. Protecting our existing green spaces is just as important as creating new ones.

Require that new city buildings be compliant with industry standards for green, energy efficient construction.
